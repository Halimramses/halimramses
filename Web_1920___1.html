While studying art and design for my bachelor’s degree, it was extremely frustrating to witness
the lack of any intersectional, interdisciplinary, and reflexive discussions. It only focused on visual
aesthetics that usually regenerated violent stereotypes about the self and the other.
In an attempt to find a creative platform that is collaborative and communal,
I started “Re-imagining Egyptology”, a program that works as a space for artists from diverse
disciplines to come together and collaboratively work, discuss, share resources and create projects
that navigate through perceived and constructed notions of Egyptian identity.
I enjoy working closely and intimately on project development
with artists and researchers, some of the artists I worked with on their projects include: Heba Khamis, Ali Zaraay and Salma Eltarzi.

The topics I am interested in are related to: urban politics, sexualities, critical approaches to photography, archival practices, potential history, queerness, collective memory, museums, Egyptian cinema and pop culture, and projects that work around the artistic process and the different forms of labor in art production.

The services I provide are: researching, refining ideas, reflections, referring to relevant references and discussing the different themes and angles of the project.

Halim Ramses is a Visual Artist & Cultural Practitioner based in Cairo, Egypt. Halim’s work explores spatial and object memory, incorporating different mediums as photography, video, workshops and story-telling performances. He engages with different archival practices trying to explore loss, silences, gaps and remembrance through series of embodiment and fictive interventions, proposing ways of being and looking towards inherited narratives and official history.
Halim’s current project Momentum, Yet Eternal Mirage (2021- ) discusses the relation of gender to photography aesthetics
and the different ways of seeing. He shares it in the form of story-telling performance with small groups of people usually while eating together.

As part of his artistic practice, he facilitates the Re-imagining Egyptology Program which is a space for artists from diverse disciplines to come together and collaboratively work, discuss, share resources and create projects that navigate through the gaps
and the silences in archives. It is an opportunity to create personal, feminist, and queer narratives that challenge the official, colonial
and chronological understandings of history.
DOTE: Cairo Lab exploring birth, death and rebirth with a small group of participants in Cairo,
by engaging with spells and the book Exiting Towards Day Light (often mistranslated as the Book of the Dead)

The themes were approached through the tangible and the fictional, working through embodiment practices, reading ancient Egyptian mythology from “the Book of the Dead”, writing and rewriting.
We worked through conversations and exchanges towards the creation of spells that encode our personal stories and manifestations that were read and shared later in a performative, discursive event open for the public, in conversation with other groups.

The lab was part of Dancing on the Edge festival’s year of listening (@dancingontheedge), and was co-facilitated with Sara Fakhry Ismail.
2022: Alexandrian Tarantella, Bsarya Gallery, Project by Nermin Nizar, Curated by Halim Ramses, Alexandria, Egypt.
The program problematizes the gaps and the silences in archives. It is an opportunity to create personal, feminist, and queer narratives that challenge the official, colonial and chronological understandings of history and question ways of doing so. Together, we create a safe platform for the local art community to raise questions collectively. During the first round, 16 art projects were produced. In the midst of Cairo’s congestion, our gatherings took place in a shared studio in Garden City. While the second round was a collaboration with Ard for Art.
